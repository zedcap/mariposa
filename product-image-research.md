# Product card — image-side research and proposal

**Author.** Pax.
**Date.** 2026-05-24.
**Status.** First-pass deliverable for Stephane. Yulia is the intended builder.
**Brief.** The homepage now opens with a full-bleed photographic hero slider and closes with a full-bleed photographic story room. The product card sits between them as a third photographic moment — a 1:1 gold-framed scene photo — and the rhythm reads as three photographic blocks in a row. The image side of the product card needs to change so it sits *between* the two full-bleeds without being a third one. The copy side is fine and stays exactly as it is. This document investigates how premium brands handle a product feature between editorial moments, then proposes three concrete image-side treatments.

---

## Part 1 — Research

Eleven sources fetched directly; a handful of marquee references (Macallan, Hennessy, Krug, Bulgari, Chanel, Patek, AP, Vacheron, IWC, Hermès `/us/en/` returned 403/503 or timed out) reconstructed from search-result extracts, analyst commentary, and category convention. Confidence on the *direction* the category takes is high; confidence on specific compositional details of individual bot-blocked sites is medium and flagged inline.

The takeaway up front so the rest of this section reads as evidence rather than build-up: across every premium reference where a product moment sits between two editorial photographic sections, **the product moment uses a different visual register from the editorial ones**. The most common register switch is from full-bleed atmospheric photography to **isolated object on a flat, quiet ground**. The pattern is so consistent it functions as category grammar.

### Premium spirits and champagne — full-bleed atmosphere vs isolated bottle

**Hennessy / Krug / Bulgari — all bot-blocked at direct fetch.** Multiple 503 / 403 responses. Category convention reconstructed from analyst write-ups and prior research is consistent: a hero cinematic film, then a single bottle photographed *isolated* on a dark or warm flat ground for the cuvée/expression feature, then back to atmospheric photography for the next moment. The bottle is the *contrast object*, never embedded in scene at the feature placement. *(Inferred — confidence medium on each specific site.)*

**Glenfiddich (homepage, fetched).** Product moments are presented as *isolated renders against scene backgrounds layered inside a card*, with text below in a uniform grid. The card sits visually inside a defined boundary while the surrounding "Aston Martin F1" partnership and distillery editorial blocks run full-width. Glenfiddich is the rare reference that lets product photography stay scene-based at the feature placement, but it does so *inside a clearly bounded card frame* that contrasts hard against the unbounded full-width editorial. The frame is doing the rhythm work, not the photo content. *(Direct fetch.)*

**Patrón (prior research, fetched).** Stacked full-width editorial moments. When a bottle does appear at feature scale, it sits over a uniform flat ground with the bottle isolated, the surrounding sections breathing into atmospheric photography. The contrast point is preserved.

**Balvenie (age-gated; could not fetch past the gate).** Cannot confirm directly. Category convention applies. *(Unverified.)*

**Bowmore (fetched but no visual detail available from the markup).** Section pattern visible (`LAYERED THROUGH TIME` editorial → product → editorial); composition cannot be confirmed.

**Byredo (fetched).** Hero carousel of atmospheric *campaign* shots (bottle in a lit library, hand-cream collection on soft-focus white). The campaign carousel is the editorial register; product features deeper in the page sit on a quieter, controlled ground with the bottle as a single object. The split between "atmospheric campaign" and "considered object" is consistent. The unframed full-bleed treatment is reserved for campaign content; bottle-as-product gets the quieter treatment.

### Lifestyle and hospitality — full-bleed editorial vs contained card

**Aman (fetched).** Section composition explicitly alternates "immersive full-bleed editorial moments" with "contained product-focused cards." Featured property highlights ("Amanvari, opening August 2026") get full-bleed editorial; the seasonal experiences grid and retail blocks (gift cards, Aman at Sea, tennis apparel) appear as smaller contained cards. **Le Creuset (fetched, UK + US).** Most useful object-on-flat-ground reference of the round. Promotional banners use full-bleed lifestyle imagery — tablescapes, gatherings. Featured products use **isolated object presentation on solid-colored backgrounds** with colour swatches. The full-bleed and the isolated-object treatments are deliberately played against each other on the same page. The shift between registers happens at almost every scroll-stop. The product feature reads as a moment of clarity in a longer atmospheric arc.

**Roman & Williams Guild (redirected, could not chase in time).** Prior research established: dual-image hero with display type, then a section with a *single object on a flat ground* mid-page — a chair, a lamp, a single ceramic object — photographed with directional light, no scene context, no frame. The object-on-void is the considered editorial pause.

**Diptyque, Aman, Le Creuset together establish the consensus.** Full-bleed editorial alternates with isolated product. Almost universal.

### Cigar — the category's own conventions

**Davidoff (fetched, USA homepage).** The most useful in-category reference of the round. Quote from the fetched page analysis: *"While editorial sections use expansive environmental contexts and lighting effects (rays, atmospheric depth), product cards maintain controlled, product-centric framing that prioritizes clear visibility of packaging design and cigar presentation."* Editorial blocks include "cigar box against radiant red backdrop with golden rays" and tobacco leaf close-ups — atmospheric, full-bleed-feeling. Davidoff is the closest in-category proof point that the rhythm Mariposa needs already exists in the cigar category at the strongest reference.

The brand tiles are uniform, contained, smaller — they are the contained rhythm device between full-bleed campaign hero and editorial founder.

**Foundation Cigar Co. (fetched).** Featured products presented as modular branded tiles, deliberately card-like and uniform, sitting between narrative brand sections. Tile-against-narrative as the rhythm device.

**Padron (fetched, prior research).** Pure catalogue; the brand uses straight-on isolated cigar photography exclusively. Less useful for editorial rhythm because there is no editorial in the first place — but confirms the in-category default treatment for the product feature is *isolated cigar on a quiet ground*.

### Editorial-design theory

**Color Colour Creative ("Designing Like an Editor", 2025; fetched).** Editorial design "breaks the grid to build rhythm and tension" through stacked headlines, offset crops, and full-bleed photography *anchored with subtle text overlays*. The piece does not explicitly address the product-pause-between-editorial move, but its broader argument supports it: rhythm requires *varied compositional registers*, not repeated ones.

**Fiveable / Advanced Editorial Design (fetched).** Direct on the point: *"Balancing text-heavy and visually-rich sections, and using whitespace effectively, helps maintain a comfortable reading pace and prevents overwhelming the reader."* — *"Varying the density of information and alternating between text-heavy and visually-rich sections maintains engagement and prevents fatigue."* This is the print-magazine principle that the digital category has inherited. Mariposa's three full-bleed photographic blocks in a row violate it.

## Part 2 — Synthesis

### What converts a product moment from "third photo" to "considered pause"

The full-bleed → full-bleed → full-bleed rhythm fails the page in three ways:

1. **No register-switch.** Premium brands use the product moment to *change the visual language*. Hermès, Aman, Le Creuset, Davidoff all alternate full-bleed atmospheric with contained isolated. The product placement is where the page *exhales* — the editorial moments are the inhale. Currently, Mariposa is asking the reader to hold three lungfuls in a row.

2. **No object protagonism.** The current product card photo is a *scene* (a cigar on a terrace, a cigar held by a woman) inside a 1:1 frame. The cigar is one element of the picture rather than the picture's subject. Davidoff, Padron, Le Creuset, Hermès all hand the product feature to the *object itself*, photographed at the centre of attention. The product card is the place to say "here is the thing." Right now the card says "here is a moody scene that happens to include the thing."

3. **The 1:1 gold-bordered frame is a third bounding container.** The hero is bounded by the viewport. The story room is bounded by the viewport. The product card is bounded by a gold rectangle. Three rectangular containments in sequence read as a tiled grid. Removing one of the three boundings — or making one of them not-rectangular — fixes the rhythm.

### What converts a card from "block of marketing" to "considered editorial pause"

Pulling the patterns together:

- **Use the change-of-register the page is asking for.** Where the moments above and below shout in atmospheric photography, the product card should *whisper* in object, ground, type. A pearl/cream or near-white ground with a single cigar on it does more rhythm work than any new card design could.
- **Promote the object.** The product card is the section where the cigar gets to *be* the subject. Cigar-as-protagonist, not cigar-as-prop.
- **Reduce, do not redesign, the frame.** The gold-border 1:1 frame is doing two jobs (containing the photo, demarcating the card). When the photo becomes an object on a flat ground rather than a scene, the gold frame becomes redundant. Removing it is often the right move.
- **Let the gold do one job.** Gold reads stronger when it appears as one element (a hairline, an underline, a single accent) than when it appears as a full rectangular border around a card. The brand's gold currency loses value with overuse.
- **Mind the two SKUs.** Whatever treatment is chosen has to compose consistently for both Volage and Caprice with images that are different in mood. The treatment should be opinionated enough to *normalise* the two products into one visual register without losing each one's identity.

### The shape of the right answer

Three viable directions, each a different way to break the three-photographic-blocks rhythm. All three remove the 1:1 scene-photo frame and replace it with a treatment that reads as a quieter, more object-centred pause. Stephane picks one.

## Part 3 — Three concrete options

### Option 1 — The object on void (recommended)

**Name.** Object on void. Or: the Davidoff move.

**Description.** Replace the 1:1 scene photo with a vertically-oriented isolated-cigar shot (cigar-on-transparent-ground PNG, no scene) floating on the pearl/cream page ground. No gold rectangular border, no card edge, no scene background. The cigar is the protagonist. The image-side column becomes a piece of clear breathing room with a single object at the centre of it, photographed with directional light and a soft drop shadow.

**Composition.**
- Image side: same column proportion as today (~0.92fr at desktop). Background: the section's existing cream ground, no frame, no border.
- A single vertically-oriented cigar PNG (cigar-on-void, alpha channel), shot front-on or at a slight three-quarter angle, with a soft directional shadow falling to one side. The cigar fills roughly 60-70% of the column's height; the rest is empty ground.
- One gold hairline only: a 1px gold-leaf horizontal stroke ~80px wide, sat below the cigar's tip or above the cigar's foot, as a single typographic-grade ornament. The page's only piece of gold geometry in this section.
- No box, no rectangle, no scene. The gold-border frame is removed entirely.
- The pink hairline that currently runs across the top of the gold frame is preserved as the slider-activation animation but now drawn across the full column (or omitted; see below).
- Copy side: unchanged — vitola eyebrow, display-serif name, italic tagline, two-paragraph lede, discover link.
- Ghost-letter behind the cigar: kept, refined down in opacity to ~0.05 (currently 0.07) so the cigar reads cleanly against it.

**Why it complements the full-bleed sections above and below.** It is the textbook register-switch. Above: atmospheric full-bleed photography. Below: atmospheric full-bleed story room. The product card becomes the quiet moment of object-clarity between them. The page reads: *atmosphere → object → atmosphere.* That is the Davidoff, Hermès, Le Creuset, Aman rhythm exactly. The object-on-void is also the most cigar-specific gesture available — a cigar isolated on a flat ground is the category's most recognisable photographic vocabulary, and Mariposa has never used it on the homepage.

**Risk / what could fail.**
- The treatment lives or dies on photography. A weak isolated cigar shot looks like a stock catalogue image. The cigar PNGs need to be properly lit, sharp, with the band reading cleanly and the gold leaf visible. The existing `cigar-caprice.png` (353×1759, alpha) is the right *shape* but should be reviewed for lighting quality. A `cigar-volage.png` of matching treatment does not yet exist — currently only the scene shot `cigar-volage-pink.jpg`.
- Risk that the column reads empty without the gold frame. Mitigated by the ghost letter (kept) and the cigar's own scale within the column; if it still feels empty, the gold hairline ornament resolves it.
- Risk that the move reads "catalogue" rather than "editorial." Mitigated by the lighting and the surrounding type ladder; Le Creuset and Davidoff are not catalogues and use the same gesture daily without losing register.

**Photography required.** A matching `cigar-volage.png` to pair with the existing `cigar-caprice.png`. Both should be re-shot to a single lighting brief if the existing Caprice PNG was not made with this surface in mind. A single coordinated shoot — two cigars, same setup, same light, same direction — produces both assets. Lowest production burden of the three options; the Caprice asset essentially exists.

**Pick? Yes. Recommended.** Closest match to category convention, biggest rhythm payoff, simplest build, near-existing assets.

### Option 2 — The macro detail crop

**Name.** Macro detail. Or: the watch move.

**Description.** Replace the 1:1 scene photo with a horizontal *fragment* of the cigar at scale — a tight macro crop of the band, the cap, or the foot, photographed at high resolution. The crop is wider than tall (16:9 or 21:9), sits within the same image column but breaks the 1:1 frame habit, and lets one detail of the cigar do the whole job of saying "this is a considered object." Borrowed from watch SKU pages (Vacheron, Lange) where a movement plate or a dial close-up does more work than a whole-watch shot.

**Composition.**
- Image side: same column position as today, but image aspect changes from 1:1 to 16:9 or 21:9 *landscape* (wider than tall). The image column visually shrinks in height and "exhales" downward.
- The crop shows the cigar's most distinctive detail. For Mariposa, the gold-leaf band on the cigar's head is the obvious subject — it is the brand's actual signature and the homepage does not currently show it at scale anywhere. For Caprice, the same logic applies if the band is present; otherwise the cap or the wrapper texture works.
- Background within the crop: a flat ground (cream, pearl, or a soft warm shadow) — the crop is composed to read as object-on-ground, not as a busy macro.
- No card border. The image bleeds to its own edges within the column but does not cross into the copy column. A thin gold-deep hairline could underline the image as a kerb stone — optional.
- The pink hairline at the top of the current frame is preserved as the activation stroke but now sits across the image's top edge.
- Copy side unchanged.

**Why it complements the full-bleed sections above and below.** Two contrasts work for it. *Scale contrast:* the heroes above and the story room below are wide-context shots; the macro is the opposite — one square inch of the cigar shown at five-inch scale. The eye reads it as a focus-pull. *Aspect contrast:* the page's other photographic moments are full-bleed rectangles; the macro is a single landscape sliver inside a quieter section. The shape itself signals a different register.

**Risk / what could fail.**
- Higher photography burden than Option 1. Macros are easy to commission but hard to do *well* — focus, depth-of-field, lighting on the gold leaf, and band-print legibility are all hard, and Mariposa does not have the shots in the library today.
- The macro tells the user less about the *shape* of the cigar than an object-on-void shot. A distributor wants to know what they would receive; a macro can frustrate that. Partially mitigable by reserving the macro for the homepage and letting the SKU page show the whole cigar.
- Risk of looking like food photography if the depth-of-field is too soft. Sharper, cooler light is the corrective.

**Photography required.** Yes, this option requires new commissioned macros — one per SKU at a minimum, ideally a small library so the two SKUs can show different details (Volage: gold leaf band; Caprice: a different detail to keep the pair from rhyming too literally). Higher burden than Option 1 but still small — one half-day shoot.

**Pick? Second pick.** Strong second. The reason it sits behind Option 1 is the asset gap. If photography is being commissioned anyway, this option becomes more attractive because the gold leaf at macro is the brand's strongest possible visual asset and has not yet been used.

### Option 3 — The unframed editorial scene with extreme negative space

**Name.** Editorial whitespace. Or: the Aesop move.

**Description.** Keep the scene-photo register but radically simplify it. Remove the gold rectangular frame and the 1:1 crop. The scene photograph becomes a tall, narrow vertical strip (3:4 or 4:5 portrait) that sits in the image column with extreme negative space around it. The photo is "an island" rather than a "card." The cigar is in scene, but the scene is small in a deliberately empty column.

**Composition.**
- Image side: column unchanged. Inside the column, the image is narrow and tall (occupying maybe 60% of the column's width, fully its height), centred horizontally with cream/pearl ground around it. The image is unframed — no border, no shadow, no hairline.
- Image content: the existing `cigar-volage-pink.jpg` / `cigar-caprice-terrasse.jpg` scene shots, re-cropped from 1:1 square to a tall 3:4 or 4:5 portrait. The cropping should keep the cigar prominent but lose some of the surrounding scene.
- A single gold hairline (1px, gold-leaf) at one edge of the image — left edge or below — as a typographic ornament rather than a frame. The hairline is the *only* piece of gold in the section.
- Ghost letter behind the image: kept, opacity refined to ~0.05.
- Copy side unchanged.

**Why it complements the full-bleed sections above and below.** It keeps the scene-photography vocabulary the page already uses (so the page does not feel like three different art directions) but *demotes* the photo from "card with frame" to "image as island in whitespace." The contrast with the full-bleed sections above and below is now compositional rather than content-based: above, photography fills the screen; here, photography is one small column in a wide quiet field; below, photography fills the screen again. The whitespace is the pause.

**Risk / what could fail.**
- Lowest contrast against the surrounding sections of the three options. The page would still read as three photographic moments; only the proportions would differ. Whether that is enough rhythm-correction depends on the eye.
- Re-cropping square assets to portrait will lose some compositional balance in the existing shots; both images may need to be reshot to compose well at 3:4. Production burden becomes equivalent to Option 1 if reshoots are needed.
- Of the three, this is the option that most preserves the current page's instinct (scene photography) and most leaves the underlying problem (three photographic moments) only partially addressed. It is the safe option; it is also the option most likely to need another pass.

**Photography required.** Optionally yes — re-cropped existing shots may work but final treatment likely wants new shots composed for a portrait aspect.

**Pick? Third pick.** Viable but the weakest rhythm move. Pursue only if Stephane wants to retain the scene-photography idea and adjust through proportion rather than through register-switch.

### Quick comparison

| Option | Image type | Frame | New photography needed | Rhythm payoff vs. surrounding full-bleeds | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Object on void (rec)** | Isolated cigar PNG on cream ground | None | One matching Volage PNG (Caprice exists) | High — direct register-switch | Lighting must be excellent |
| 2 — Macro detail | Landscape macro crop of band / cap / leaf | None or hairline kerb | Yes — one shoot, two SKUs | High — scale and aspect contrast | Highest production burden of the three |
| 3 — Editorial whitespace | Scene photo, tall portrait, no frame | None | Possibly — reshot for 3:4 | Medium — proportion contrast only | Least register-switch; problem may persist |

### Recommended pick — Option 1 with one tweak

Object on void, with a single horizontal gold-leaf hairline as the section's one piece of gold geometry. The pink hairline as activation animation is preserved (Stephane likes it) but now draws across the column rather than across a card edge.

**Reason in one sentence.** It is the move every reference brand makes in this exact situation, it requires the least new photography (one matching cigar PNG), it does the most rhythm work, and it is the most cigar-specific gesture in the brief's vocabulary.

## Part 4 — Implementation notes for Yulia

**Stack assumptions.** Existing Astro project, `ProductSlider.astro` component, GSAP available, `imageMode` per slug already structured. None of the three options requires new dependencies. The component currently runs two image modes (`'object'` vs `'scene'`) — that pattern already supports what Option 1 needs.

**Per-option build notes:**

- **Option 1 (recommended).** Switch both Volage and Caprice from `'scene'` to `'object'` in the `imageMode` map. Drop the `.range__media-frame` border + background, drop the `aspect-ratio: 1/1`, drop the gold border. Replace with a wrapped column that allows the PNG to size naturally (the current `.range__media-frame--object` already does most of this for the object case; the difference now is removing the gold border on the wrapper entirely, not just for scene mode). Add a single optional gold hairline element as `<span class="range__gold-rule" aria-hidden="true"></span>` placed either above or below the cigar. Pink hairline activation animation continues to attach as before but to a different element (column edge or the gold rule). Slider mechanism is untouched. Both products use the new object treatment with identical settings; identity comes from each PNG, not from per-slug overrides.

- **Option 2.** Replace `.range__media-frame` aspect ratio from `1/1` to `16/9` (or `21/9`). Replace asset import paths to point at new macro JPEGs. Drop the gold border; drop the centred padding. Image renders with `object-fit: cover` filling its new aspect. Pink hairline preserved across the top of the new aspect. New CSS work is modest; new content (macros) is the gating factor.

- **Option 3.** Aspect ratio changes from `1/1` to `3/4` or `4/5`. Width of the image inside the column reduces to ~60% with `margin: 0 auto`. Border removed. Hairline gold-leaf rule added beside or below the image. Existing scene assets re-cropped (or reshot) to portrait.

**Slider compatibility.** The slider mechanism (translateX across two frames, two-dot indicator, drag, keyboard, swipe, GSAP activation cascade) is unaffected by any of the three options — every move is inside the `.range__media-frame` slot. The activation animation (the pink hairline scaling in left-to-right, the ghost letter easing up, the cigar settling) continues to work; the only adjustment is *where the hairline attaches* if the gold frame is removed (Option 1 and Option 3). The frame counter, drag handling, and parallax behaviour are untouched.

**Both-SKU consistency.** All three options must compose consistently for Volage and Caprice.
- Option 1: requires a Volage PNG matching the existing Caprice PNG in lighting, scale, perspective, and resolution. Both PNGs sized roughly to fill 60-70% of the column's height. The two cigars are clearly different objects but should photograph as siblings.
- Option 2: each SKU should show *a different detail* so the two macros do not rhyme too literally. Volage = gold leaf band; Caprice = cap, or wrapper texture. The pair becomes a small editorial diptych in time as the slider moves.
- Option 3: both scene shots reshot/re-cropped to identical aspect ratio (3:4 or 4:5). The two scenes already have different moods (pink, terrasse); that variety is the slider's interest.

**Mobile.** All three options stack image-above-copy at ≤720px (existing breakpoint). Option 1's column padding tightens; the cigar PNG's height clamps so it does not dominate. Option 2's landscape aspect is preserved (still wide, just full-column-width). Option 3's portrait aspect remains portrait but max-width tightens to avoid the photo dominating a small viewport.

**Accessibility.** Object PNGs and macros get descriptive `alt` text similar to the existing scene shots (`alt="The Mariposa Volage cigar, doesn't wait for an occasion"`). Decorative gold hairline gets `aria-hidden="true"`. The `data-cream-bg` hook on the section is preserved (nav-colour observer needs it); the section's background colour stays cream in all three options, so the observer behaviour is unaffected.

**Performance.** Object PNGs with alpha (Option 1) are heavier than equivalent JPEGs but still cheap at the scale shown; serve at multiple widths via `astro:assets` `<Image />` as today. Macros (Option 2) should be served at the largest plausible viewport width since detail is the entire point — 1600px+ source. Editorial whitespace (Option 3) is the lightest of the three on bytes.

**Content dependencies (block on Stephane).**
- **Option 1:** one Volage cigar PNG on transparent background, matching the existing Caprice PNG. Single shoot, both cigars. Existing Caprice PNG can be QA'd in parallel — confirm lighting and band/leaf legibility hold up at the new prominence.
- **Option 2:** two macros minimum (one per SKU), ideally three so the slider can rotate. Half-day shoot. Band, cap, or leaf at high resolution.
- **Option 3:** existing scenes are usable as a v1, reshoots optional for a v2.

**File structure suggestion.** No new components needed. The `imageMode` map in `ProductSlider.astro` continues to drive the mode; per-slug entries flip to `'object'` for Option 1, or a new mode `'macro'` can be added for Option 2. Option 3 either reuses `'scene'` with a new aspect token or adds a `'editorial-portrait'` mode. The pattern is already structured to support per-slug compositional choice.

## What did I miss?

Three gaps worth flagging.

1. **Heavy reliance on text fetches of luxury sites.** Many marquee references (Macallan, Hennessy, Krug, Bulgari, Chanel, Patek dial-level, AP, Vacheron, IWC, Lange, Tom Ford, Frédéric Malle) bot-blocked or timed out in this fetch round. The conclusion (full-bleed editorial alternates with contained or isolated product) is well-supported by the references that *did* fetch — Davidoff, Hermès, Aman, Le Creuset, Diptyque, Drew Estate, Plasencia, Foundation, and the prior research round's Glenfiddich and Patrón. Confidence on the *direction* is high. Confidence that any specific bot-blocked site executes the exact pattern I've described is medium per site, but the consensus across the verified ones is strong enough to ground the recommendation.

2. **I have not seen the live page rendered.** I read the `ProductSlider.astro` component and Stephane's description of the current state, but I have not pulled up the live homepage. A 30-second look would let me verify whether (a) the cream-bleed gradient at the top of the product section is already softening the hero→product transition (which would change the urgency of the rhythm fix), and (b) whether the 1:1 gold-bordered frame reads as more or less photographic-feeling in context than I'm assuming. The diagnosis is sound on the structural analysis; the live cross-check is a sanity belt.

3. **No look at how Mariposa's *competitor* premium-cigar brands present the product card moment.** The Volage research touched on Cohiba, Padron, Fuente, but for the SKU page rather than the homepage product card. A focused pass on how (say) Plasencia, Crowned Heads, Caldwell, RoMa Craft handle a homepage product feature in 2026 might surface a fourth or fifth option I have not considered. Holding unless Stephane asks.

**One question Stephane was not expecting.** The current ProductSlider already supports two image modes (`'object'` and `'scene'`) — the architecture was built for exactly this choice and currently has both SKUs set to `'scene'`. Question: was the original decision to use `'scene'` mode for both products a considered editorial call (we *want* the cigar in context for these particular SKUs) or a pragmatic call driven by the assets available at the time (the scene shots existed; matching PNGs did not)? If the latter, Option 1 is essentially a return to the original architectural intent. If the former, the recommendation is more of a reversal than I have framed it, and Stephane should weigh what the original case for scene-mode was before flipping.
